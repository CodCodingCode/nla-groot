\begin{abstract}
Vision-language-action (VLA) policies such as NVIDIA's GR00T expose a
high-dimensional Qwen3-VL backbone hidden state \(\hvec\) that is
causally upstream of the action head. There is no standard way to read
\(\hvec\) out as natural language or to write back into it from
language. We adapt the Natural Language Autoencoder (NLA)
recipe~\citep{frasertaliente2026nla} to this setting and release
\code{nla-groot}, an open-source implementation that (i) extracts
per-token activations from GR00T's backbone, (ii) trains a paired
\emph{activation verbalizer} \AV{} (\(\hvec \to y\)) and
\emph{activation reconstructor} \AR{} (\(y \to \hhat\)) with joint
supervised fine-tuning, with an optional GRPO phase that uses \AR{} as
a learned reconstruction reward, and (iii) injects \AR{}'s output back
into the live policy server as a backbone steering perturbation in
LIBERO closed-loop simulation. We propose a three-axis evaluation
protocol that combines offline reconstruction, a multimodal LLM
grounding judge, and a closed-loop steerability A/B test. We report a
pilot study on LIBERO Goal in which our V3 checkpoint attains a
retrieval margin of \(0.124\) and \(100\%\) caption
\emph{appropriateness}, but only \(8.3\%\) anti-template specificity,
\(41.7\%\) grounding, and a zero correct-minus-wrong steering gap on
the single task tested. Reconstruction metrics alone would call this
run a success; the full protocol does not. We propose this protocol,
together with the ``shorthand template collapse'' failure mode it
surfaces, as a prerequisite for future NLA-style interpretability work
on VLA policies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Recent vision-language-action (VLA) models pair a vision-language
backbone with a diffusion or flow-matching action head and act
end-to-end on physical robots~\citep{nvidia2025gr00t,
brohan2023rt2,kim2024openvla}. As these models scale, a question
becomes pressing: what does the policy represent internally about the
task it is about to execute, in a form that a human can read and
modify? Interpretability tooling for language models, including
probes~\citep{alain2017linear,belinkov2022probing}, sparse
autoencoders~\citep{cunningham2023sparseautoencoders,bricken2023monosemanticity},
and activation steering~\citep{turner2023actadd,zou2023repe}, has not
yet been adapted to the policy setting, where downstream behavior is
itself an evaluation signal.

\paragraph{The NLA recipe applied to a VLA.}
\citet{frasertaliente2026nla} show that an LM hidden state can be
encoded into text and decoded back: a small \emph{activation
verbalizer} (\AV) is trained to caption an injected activation, and a
paired \emph{activation reconstructor} (\AR) is trained to invert the
caption back into activation space. This produces both an
interpretability artifact (the caption) and a causal handle (the
reconstructed vector). We ask whether the same recipe transfers to a
VLA backbone, in which the hidden state is intended to carry scene and
task information that the action head consumes.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{System.} An open-source implementation
    (\code{nla-groot}) that extracts GR00T backbone activations
    (Qwen3-VL, 16-layer truncation, \(d{=}2048\)), labels them with a
    multimodal teacher, jointly trains \AV{} and \AR{} on top of a
    Qwen3-4B-Instruct base, and exposes \AR{}'s output as a live
    backbone steering vector in the official GR00T LIBERO policy
    server (\S\ref{sec:method}).
  \item \textbf{Evaluation protocol.} Three complementary axes:
    offline reconstruction (FVE, cosine, retrieval), a multimodal LLM
    judge on cached frames (axes B and C), and a closed-loop sim A/B
    test with matched, mismatched, and null caption arms. Results are
    aggregated into a single PASS/WARN/FAIL scorecard
    (\S\ref{sec:eval}).
  \item \textbf{Pilot study.} On a LIBERO 4-suite combined corpus, our
    best checkpoint passes reconstruction metrics but fails grounding
    and steering. We characterize this ``shorthand template collapse''
    failure mode, show that retrieval margin and reconstruction cosine
    alone are insufficient as a success criterion, and document a
    train-deploy distribution gap that limits behavioral steering
    (\S\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Thesis.} \emph{NLA-style autoencoding is a viable approach
to VLA interpretability, but only behind a multi-axis protocol;
reconstruction metrics in isolation are misleading.}

\section{Background and related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Vision-language-action policies.}
GR00T~\citep{nvidia2025gr00t} couples a Cosmos-Reason2-2B / Qwen3-VL
backbone with a diffusion action head. Only the first
\code{SELECT\_LAYER}\(=\!16\) decoder layers are retained at
deployment, so the policy reads a 2048-dimensional hidden state at
layer 16. Related VLAs include RT-2~\citep{brohan2023rt2},
OpenVLA~\citep{kim2024openvla}, and Octo~\citep{octo2024}. We focus on
GR00T because its backbone-head split is explicit and its checkpoints
are publicly available.

\paragraph{Activation interpretability.}
Linear probes recover task-relevant subspaces~\citep{alain2017linear},
and sparse autoencoders factor activations into approximately
monosemantic features~\citep{cunningham2023sparseautoencoders,
bricken2023monosemanticity, templeton2024scaling}. NLA
\citep{frasertaliente2026nla} complements these methods by producing
free-text explanations \(y=\AV(\hvec)\) together with a faithfulness
anchor \(\hhat=\AR(y)\). \code{nla-groot} ports this recipe to a VLA
backbone.

\paragraph{Activation steering.}
Activation editing has produced controllable behavior in language
models via linear directions~\citep{turner2023actadd, zou2023repe,
liu2024incontext, panickssery2024steering}. Behavioral steering of
robot policies is less studied, and closed-loop simulation is the
appropriate validation setting when the model is a policy. We treat
\AR{}'s output as a candidate steering vector and measure whether it
changes downstream behavior on LIBERO.

\paragraph{Robotic simulation benchmarks.}
LIBERO~\citep{liu2023libero} is a suite of MuJoCo-based manipulation
tasks with public assets. GR00T provides posttrained checkpoints per
LIBERO sub-suite (e.g.\ \code{libero\_goal}), which we use as the
closed-loop ground truth.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Data: extraction and labeling}

Per-token backbone activations are captured by a forward hook on the
GR00T \code{backbone\_features} tensor, before the post-VL LayerNorm
and the cross-attention to the action head (the layer spec is
documented in \code{src/nla/layer\_spec.py}). For each LeRobot episode
and step we record \(\hvec_i \in \mathbb{R}^{2048}\) at three position
types (text, image-patch, image-summary; see
\citet{frasertaliente2026nla}). A multimodal teacher (GPT-class) is
given the raw camera frame(s), instruction, and token-position
metadata, and emits a structured bullet-point caption that we treat as
the gold target for supervised training.

A per-corpus scalar \(\alpha\) is estimated as the 75th percentile of
\(\|\hvec\|_2\) and used for both volumetric calibration of injection
and as the unit of MSE in \AR{} training. For the LIBERO 4-suite combined
corpus \(\alpha \approx 203.98\).

\subsection{Architecture: \AV{} and \AR{}}

Both \AV{} and \AR{} are LoRA fine-tunes (rank 32, \(\alpha=64\), full
Qwen attention/MLP target set, bfloat16) of \code{Qwen/Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507}.

\paragraph{\AV{}: activation \(\to\) text.}
The prompt template contains a single \code{<|act\_slot|>} token. At
forward time we tokenize the prompt, embed the IDs, then overwrite the
slot embedding with \(\alpha \cdot \mathrm{normalize}(\mathbf{W}_p
\hvec)\) where \(\mathbf{W}_p \in \mathbb{R}^{h \times 2048}\) is a
learnable projector. Loss is cross-entropy on the gold bullet caption.

\paragraph{\AR{}: text \(\to\) activation.}
The base LM is physically truncated to its first 16 layers (matching
GR00T's \code{SELECT\_LAYER}). A linear head reads the last non-pad
hidden state of the prompt
``\emph{Summary of the following text:}~\(\langle\)text\(\rangle\)\emph{
\(\langle\)summary\(\rangle\)}''
and predicts \(\hhat / \alpha \in \mathbb{R}^{2048}\). Training loss is
mean-squared error in \(\alpha\)-scaled space, plus an InfoNCE
contrastive term with mined hard negatives (top-\(k\) cosine, \(k{=}4\)).

\subsection{Training}

\paragraph{Joint SFT.}
\AV{} and \AR{} are updated on the same batches, so that \AR{} sees
\AV{}'s gold-text targets and \AV{} cannot drift independently. The
main configuration (run \code{libero\_4suite\_v3}) uses 15{,}000 steps
with batch size \(4\), learning rate \(10^{-4}\), 500-step warmup,
cosine schedule, \(\mathrm{ar\_av\_mix\_max}=0.4\) (scheduled mixing of
AR-on-AV-sampled-text past 30\% of training, used to close the
train-deploy text gap), and held-out fraction \(0.05\) with an
episode-level split.

\paragraph{Optional GRPO phase.}
After SFT, \AV{} can be further trained by GRPO with the frozen \AR{} as
the reward model: for each \(\hvec\) we sample \(K\) captions, score
each by \(-\|\hvec/\alpha - \AR(y)\|^2\), and apply a within-group
advantage policy gradient with a Schulman-k3 KL to a frozen reference
\AV{}.

\subsection{Steering: language \(\to\) live policy}

At inference, a user supplies a short bullet caption \(y\). We compute
\(\hhat=\AR(y)\) and inject it into the live GR00T policy server by
replacing or blending the backbone token features at one of three
placements: a single \code{image\_patch} token, \emph{all} image-patch
tokens (\code{image\_patch\_all}), or the image-summary token. A scalar
\code{blend}\(\in[0,2]\) controls intensity. The injection is wired
through \code{src/nla/steering/sim\_policy\_wrapper.py} so the official
LIBERO sim client sees no difference apart from modified backbone
features on every \code{get\_action} call.

\section{Evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:eval}

We evaluate each checkpoint on three independent axes. A single
scorecard (\code{scripts/eval/build\_v3\_scorecard.py}) emits
PASS/WARN/FAIL per metric against pre-registered thresholds.

\subsection{Axis 1: Offline reconstruction and discriminability}

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Closed-loop cosine}: \(\cos(\hvec,
    \AR(\AV(\hvec)))\), computed without teacher forcing, in order to
    penalize \AV{} captions that \AR{} cannot invert.
  \item \textbf{Retrieval margin} and \textbf{retrieval@\(K\)}: matched
    versus cross-pair cosine on \(\AR(\AV(\hvec))\) against a held-out
    pool. These metrics detect non-discriminative \(\hhat\), i.e.\
    template collapse.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Axis 2: Multimodal LLM judge}

The script \code{scripts/eval/llm\_judge\_av\_captions.py} re-renders
the original frame, supplies the multimodal judge with both the frame
and the caption, and scores three rubrics: (A) format and length,
(B) \emph{grounding} (does the caption describe what is in the image,
rather than a generic template), and (C) \emph{appropriateness}
(does the caption have the expected type for this position). Gold
(teacher) captions on the same frames define a calibration ceiling.
We report both gold and \AV{} pass rates and the gap between them.

\subsection{Axis 3: Closed-loop steerability A/B}

A closed-loop steerability A/B test
(\code{scripts/eval/closed\_loop\_sim\_ab.py} and
\code{scripts/eval/steerability\_eval.py}) runs LIBERO Goal episodes on
the official GR00T checkpoint under three caption arms per task:
\emph{baseline} (no steering), \emph{correct} (caption that matches
the scene and instruction), and \emph{wrong} (a contradictory
caption). The primary metric is \(\Delta_{cw} =
\mathrm{succ}_{\text{correct}} - \mathrm{succ}_{\text{wrong}}\); we
register \(\Delta_{cw} \geq +5\)pp as the PASS threshold. Per-object
displacement and minimum end-effector distance are logged for failure
analysis.

\code{put\_the\_bowl\_on\_the\_plate}, with three seeds

\subsection{Closed-loop steerability}

We sweep three steering prompts and two AR variants
(\code{libero\_goal\_pilot} and \code{libero\_4suite\_v3}) across three
seeds (Table~\ref{tab:steer}, distilled from
\code{data/eval/steerability\_v1\_vs\_v3/metrics.json}). The baseline
policy solves the task on every seed
(\code{success\_any\_rate}\(=1.0\)). Every steering condition prevents
task completion (\code{success\_any\_rate}\(=0.0\)). Steering therefore
has a consistent behavioral effect, but the effect is symmetric: the
correct prompt (\code{bowl\_plate}) and the contradictory prompts
(\code{wine\_rack}, \code{bowl\_stove}) both block the task. This
gives \(\Delta_{cw}=0\) and rules out any semantic claim from this
pilot. The per-object displacement and end-effector traces in
Figure~\ref{fig:displacement} show that all steered conditions collapse
the bowl's displacement to approximately \(0.07\)m, compared with
\(0.145\)m at baseline. In other words, steering reduces motion
uniformly rather than redirecting it toward the prompted object.

\subsection{Discussion of results}

We summarize three findings.

\paragraph{(i) Reconstruction passes; behavior does not.}
The retrieval margin and appropriateness rate, considered in
isolation, would read as a successful training run. The grounding and
anti-template scores from the judge, together with the symmetric
LIBERO-Goal failure, contradict this reading. The protocol therefore
serves its purpose: reporting reconstruction metrics alone would have
led to an overclaim.

\paragraph{(ii) Shorthand template collapse persists in V3.}
The 8.3\% anti-template rate on the 4-suite holdout means that
\(\AV{}\) captions are largely indistinguishable from a corpus-mean
template. This is the same failure mode reported for the earlier
\code{droid\_100ep\_v2\_nce} run (V2 post-mortem in
\code{docs/sft\_plan/06\_v2\_postmortem\_v3\_rerun.md}). The V3
mitigations (\code{ar\_av\_mix\_max}, InfoNCE hard negatives, and an
episode-level split) narrow the gap but do not close it.

\paragraph{(iii) Steering changes behavior, but not yet semantically.}
Every steered condition blocks task completion, so the perturbation
clearly has effect, but the identity of the prompt does not appear to
determine what the policy attends to. We attribute this to two
factors: (a) the train-deploy distribution gap for \AR{}, which is
trained on gold-style bullet captions and deployed on hand-written
ones; and (b) the \code{image\_patch\_all} placement at
\code{blend}\(=\!1.0\), which is a coarse intervention, consistent
with the open-loop probe in
\code{scripts/eval/nla\_steer\_quant\_probe.py}.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Single task, small sample.} The closed-loop simulation
    numbers above cover three seeds on one LIBERO Goal task. We treat
    them as a feasibility study rather than a benchmark. A multi-task
    LIBERO sweep with per-prompt affordance scoring would be required
    before any quantitative claim about semantic steering.
  \item \textbf{Teacher and $\hvec$ confound.} The multimodal teacher
    sees the original frame and may describe scene attributes that are
    only weakly present in \(\hvec\). The SFT loss therefore does not
    correspond exactly to ``what \(\hvec\) encodes''. This is
    inherited from the NLA recipe and motivates the judge's grounding
    axis.
  \item \textbf{AR train-deploy gap.} \AR{} is trained on gold-style
    bullet captions, whereas user-written steering prompts are
    out-of-distribution. The \code{ar\_av\_mix\_max} schedule reduces
    but does not eliminate this gap.
  \item \textbf{Single backbone, single layer.} Activations are
    extracted at layer 16 (\code{SELECT\_LAYER}) before the post-VL
    LayerNorm. Other choices (\code{vl\_embeds}, DiT mid-block) are
    documented in \code{src/nla/layer\_spec.py} but are not ablated
    here.
  \item \textbf{Judge as oracle.} The grounding judge is itself a
    large multimodal model and may be miscalibrated. We partially
    address this by pairing every \AV{} score with a gold score on
    identical frames.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion and outlook}
\label{sec:discussion}

\code{nla-groot} shows that the NLA recipe is implementable on a
production VLA backbone, and that the resulting checkpoint can be
placed in the loop of a public closed-loop simulator. The principal
contribution of this paper is the evaluation protocol rather than any
single result. A multi-axis scorecard with pre-registered thresholds
and a behavioral arm distinguishes apparently successful runs from
genuinely successful ones.

A natural next step is the GRPO phase against the AR-as-reward signal,
for which the supporting code is in place
(\code{scripts/training/run\_grpo.py}) but no results are reported
here. GRPO with a judge-blended reward should directly address
template collapse, since judge rewards penalize generic captions in a
way that the supervised cross-entropy objective does not.
